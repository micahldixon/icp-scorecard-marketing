The 5 Pillars of Tier Prediction

1. Builder Identity
Do they see themselves as building something long-term, or solving a short-term problem? S-Tier says "I want to scale to the sky." C-Tier says "If you can help me with this one thing."
2. Ownership Posture
Do they own their mistakes and gaps, or externalize blame? S-Tier: "I've been the bottleneck." C-Tier: "It's not working like it used to" (market's fault).
3. Action Evidence
Have they already taken action BEFORE the call? S-Tier consumed courses, filled out planners, built lead magnets. C-Tier is "exploring" and "toying between" options.
4. Community Orientation
Do they naturally think about contributing to others, or only extracting for themselves? S-Tier offers podcast testimonials and wants staff to learn. C-Tier says "I don't want teammates."
5. Decision Sovereignty
Can they make the investment decision autonomously? S-Tier decides within the call or within hours. C-Tier needs spouse, accountant, or partner approval and delays weeks.

S-TIER Green Light Signals

If you hear 5+ of these on a call, you likely have an S-Tier prospect (The Architect).

  • ✓
    Pre-call engagement: Already consumed free content, filled out business planner, or researched the program before the discovery call.
    Matt: "I can't wait to get inside your guys' funnel. I've been consuming all of them." Victor M: "We already pre-decided we were going to work with you guys."
  • ✓
    Specific numbers without prompting: Quotes exact MRR, margins, pipeline values, and growth targets without being asked to calculate.
    Harry: "$20k MRR... 6 ghostwriting clients... $3K euros/month retainers."
  • ✓
    Self-diagnosed bottleneck: Identifies their own constraint with clarity and ownership rather than vague frustration.
    Matt: "I've been a bit of a bottleneck in terms of skill." Harry: "The bottleneck is making our offer scalable."
  • ✓
    Systems language: Talks in frameworks, processes, KPIs. Asks about dashboards, tracking, and operational efficiency.
    Matt: "I saw that dashboard and I was just bouncing off the walls."
  • ✓
    12+ month vision: Articulates goals beyond the 90-day sprint; sees the program as acceleration, not a magic bullet.
    Victor M: "I don't think I want to sell. I just love building something." Target: $100k MRR in 12-14 months.
  • ✓
    ROI framing (not cost framing): Immediately calculates how the investment pays for itself through client wins.
    Harry: "This offer is only 5k. The ROI on that 5k is going to be incredible."
  • ✓
    Community contribution offers: Voluntarily offers to create content, record testimonials, refer others, or bring staff to learn.
    Harry: "My goal is to be your best testimonial." Matt: "I'm prepared to invest and get my staff going through calls."
  • ✓
    Direct feedback / no-BS filter: Gives honest, constructive criticism about your marketing or sales process mid-call.
    Harry: "Every single testimonial starts 'Before working with Nigel.' That turned me off."
  • ✓
    Ethical awareness: Has turned down money or pivoted away from something that didn't feel right.
    Victor M: "It didn't feel ethically giving advice on something that I haven't done myself."
  • ✓
    Quick, autonomous decision-making: Decides within the call or commits to a firm timeline measured in hours, not weeks.
    Matt: "Within 60 minutes, I'm prepared to give a firm yes or no."

A-TIER Positive Signals (with caveats)

Solid prospects (The Optimizer) who need one more push to be great. Listen for the "ceiling + self-awareness" combo.

  • ✓
    Revenue exists but has a ceiling: Making money, but growth has plateaued or one channel is maxed out.
    Manuel: 70k MRR but 20% margins. Daniel: 15-20k CAD but 3 clients only. Mischa: "$2,100/month but knows she should charge 3-5x."
  • ✓
    "I know I'm underselling myself": Self-aware about pricing problems and ready to change with guidance.
    Mischa: "I personally know that I'm massively underselling myself... my prices are really low." Henry: "My closing rates are way too high. I need to jack up my prices."
  • ✓
    Methodical decision-making: Not impulsive, but not avoidant. Asks specific questions, wants to understand before committing.
    Daniel: "Can you send me a breakdown? A TLDR?"
  • !
    Compares options openly (not a red flag here): Evaluating competitors means they're serious buyers, not tire-kickers.
    Henry: Comparing Six Figure Creative vs. Undeniable. Manuel: Already has a 1:1 mentor.
  • ✓
    Operational thinking visible: Has team, systems, processes — just needs optimization, not creation.
    Manuel: COO structure, cluster model, health trackers. Henry: GoHighLevel templates, VA in Philippines, ClickUp.

B-TIER Caution Signals

Not bad people (The Foundation Builder), but will require disproportionate coaching resources relative to outcomes.

  • !
    Pain-driven, not vision-driven: Running FROM something (bad job, stagnation) rather than TOWARD something specific.
    David: "The cost of staying where you are is greater than the cost of moving forward." (Fear framing, not aspiration framing.)
  • !
    Explicit confidence deficit: States they "struggle with confidence" or need external reassurance to take action.
    Mark had to tell David: "I want you to approach these months with an insane amount of confidence" because David explicitly struggles with it.
  • !
    Compliance without internalization: Follows instructions precisely but doesn't seem to understand the "why."
    David: "Yeah... Yes... Yep" throughout the call. Takes notes, follows steps, but doesn't ask strategic questions.
  • !
    Complacency once goals met: Stops pushing once they hit a comfort level rather than stretching further.
    Victor D: "As long as I have food, water, and dancing, I'm pretty much good." Coaches had to "challenge" him past $50k goal.
  • !
    Delegation of community participation: Sends team members to calls instead of attending personally.
    Victor D: Plans to send operations manager to CFO call, "might listen to recording."

C-TIER Red Flag Signals (First 15 Minutes)

If you catch 3+ of these in the first 15 minutes, strongly consider disqualifying (The Explorer).

  • ✗
    "I already know everything": Pre-closing language that dismisses the discovery process. They've already decided what they need (usually wrong).
    Frank: "I already know everything about you guys because of Victor." Eric: "I'm familiar with all of the strategy... it's information I already have access to."
  • ✗
    External decision dependency: Can't commit without spouse, partner, or accountant approval. Decision sovereignty is absent.
    Andrew: "I need to chat to my wife." Cassie: "I need to check who paid" before committing. Jason: "I have a 35k bill I'm about to pay."
  • ✗
    Conditional language patterns: Heavy use of "if," "maybe," "probably," "we'll see" when discussing commitment.
    Frank: "If I'm impressed, I will buy." Jason: "If the price is right." Andrew: "Maybe a month from now I can..."
  • ✗
    Bandwidth already maxed: Currently overextended with job, clients, side projects. No capacity for a 90-day sprint.
    Frank: "I have all my one-on-ones today." Jenny: "My capacity is full enough, just recently a little bit for business development." Andrew: 50%+ employed elsewhere.
  • ✗
    Wants tactics, not transformation: Seeks one specific deliverable (stage access, lead gen tactic) rather than comprehensive growth.
    Frank: "If you can help me get strategic on stage, I will pay you money for that." Eric: "I'm looking for really tactics... scripts."
  • ✗
    Boom-bust revenue history: Pattern of spiking then crashing, multiple pivots, volatile career trajectory.
    Jason: $80k agency → bust → $100k → $5k → $30k → repeat over 20 years. Cassie: $30k → $10k (67% decline).
  • ✗
    Shopping around explicitly: Mentions other programs they're evaluating in a hedging way (not comparing = deciding).
    Andrew: "Another agency owner is also reaching out." Eric: Questioning how program differs from info he already has.
  • ✗
    "It's not working like it used to": Blames market changes rather than owning their own stagnation.
    Cassie: "I don't know if I've lost my mojo... it's not working like it did." (Externalizing responsibility.)
  • ✗
    Controlling / dominating the call: Takes over, interrupts, tests the seller, positions themselves as equal or superior.
    Frank: "You are my vendor." "Is this recording for me or just for you?" Constant self-aggrandizing stories.
  • ✗
    Anti-community language: Explicitly rejects collaboration, teamwork, or shared learning.
    Frank: "I don't want teammates. I want to do this by myself."

Psychological Archetypes by Tier

S-TIER — The Architect

Core Identity

Sees themselves as building something meaningful for the long term. Not chasing exits or quick cash. Growth is intrinsic to their identity, not a means to an end.

Relationship to Failure

Owns mistakes openly and specifically. Uses past failures as data for current decisions. No shame, no hiding — just pragmatic course correction.

Investment Frame

Views coaching/mentorship as "paying people better than us." Calculates ROI on the call. Sees the program as acceleration, not rescue.

Social Orientation

Naturally collaborative. Brings team to learn, offers testimonials voluntarily, attends events. Thinks "how can this help everyone" not just "how does this help me."

A-TIER — The Optimizer

Core Identity

Has a working business and knows it could be much bigger. Self-aware about the ceiling but needs external frameworks and permission to break through.

Relationship to Failure

Recognizes mistakes but may need external validation that change is the right move. "I know my prices are wrong" but hasn't fixed it yet.

Investment Frame

Methodical: wants to understand the full picture before committing. Not impulsive, not avoidant. Asks "what does success look like specifically?"

Social Orientation

Interested in community for learning and validation. Will engage moderately. Wants to be around people 1-2 steps ahead. Won't dominate but will contribute.

B-TIER — The Foundation Builder

Core Identity

Either pre-revenue and escaping a bad situation (David) or comfortable and complacent (Victor D). Both need external structure to drive action. Identity not yet anchored in entrepreneurship.

Relationship to Failure

Swings between fear and excitement. Confidence is externally sourced — coach must provide it. Doesn't yet have the internal resilience loop of higher tiers.

Investment Frame

Pays without much objection (David) or negotiates based on capacity (Victor D). Neither calculates ROI — they're buying hope or structure, not acceleration.

Social Orientation

Seeks community out of loneliness (David) or is ambivalent about it (Victor D). Neither will drive culture. Need to be explicitly matched with peers at similar stages.

C-TIER — The Explorer

Core Identity

Sees coaching as a service transaction, not a transformation. Pre-decided what they need (usually a narrow tactic). Views the relationship as vendor-client, not mentor-mentee.

Relationship to Failure

Externalizes blame. "The market changed." "It's not working like it used to." "I need to check if my clients paid." Never: "I created this problem."

Investment Frame

May pay quickly (Frank paid $15k fast) but the money is conditional on specific narrow deliverables. If the promised outcome doesn't materialize on their accelerated timeline, they'll request refund.

Social Orientation

Either dominating (Frank controls calls, positions as equal) or extractive (take tactics, don't contribute). Will not build culture. Some are warm but passive (Jenny, Cassie) — nice people, wrong program.

Sales Playbook by Predicted Tier

S-TIER The Architect — Close Fast, Protect the Relationship

  • ✓
    Don't oversell. They've already pre-decided. Match their energy, validate their instinct, and remove friction. Harry and Victor M were pre-committed before the call ended.
  • ✓
    Use their language back. When they say "I want to be your best testimonial" — that's your close. "Then let's make that happen. Here's how we start."
  • ✓
    Give them implementation details, not inspiration. They don't need motivation. They need "here's the dashboard, here's week 1, here's what you'll build."
  • ✓
    Accommodate creative deal structures. Harry negotiated podcast + mastermind + split payment. Architects will find creative ways to make it work. Let them.
  • ✓
    Assign them community roles early. They'll naturally become culture-builders. Give them micro-responsibilities (welcome new members, share case studies) within the first week.

A-TIER The Optimizer — Build Confidence, Show the Framework

  • ✓
    Give them the pricing breakthrough on the call. When they say "I know I'm underselling myself," that's your opening. Show them what proper pricing looks like. This creates an immediate win that they attribute to you.
  • ✓
    Show specific peer examples. "Thierry was in your exact situation... here's what happened in 90 days." Optimizers respond to proof, not promises.
  • ✓
    Don't pressure the close. Let them compare. If your call was solid, they'll come back. Daniel and Manuel both needed processing time — and that's fine for Optimizers.
  • ✓
    Address the "other mentor" objection directly. Manuel already had a 1:1 mentor. Frame it as "complementary, not competing" — community + systems vs. 1:1 tactical advice.

B-TIER The Foundation Builder — Set Expectations, Create Guardrails

  • !
    Be honest about the work required. If they're pre-revenue (David), make sure they understand the 90-day sprint requires significant time and action. Don't let excitement mask capacity constraints.
  • !
    Pair them with stage-appropriate peers immediately. Mark explicitly told David to connect with Tom and Lewis. Foundation Builders will feel lost among Architect/Optimizer achievers.
  • !
    Build in accountability checkpoints. Foundation Builders need more structure. Weekly check-ins, specific deliverables, clear milestones. Without these, they'll drift.
  • !
    Consider whether enrollment serves them. If someone is clearly pre-revenue with no business model, the honest thing may be "come back when you have your first client." This protects your community AND the prospect.

C-TIER The Explorer — Disqualify with Respect

  • ✗
    Don't chase Explorer revenue. $5-10k from someone who will request a refund, drain coaching resources, and potentially damage community culture is a net negative. Walk away.
  • ✗
    Redirect with dignity. "Based on what you've shared, I think a 1:1 consultant specializing in [their specific need] would serve you better right now. We can revisit in 6 months."
  • ✗
    If you do enroll them, document expectations. Clear written expectations about participation, action requirements, and what refund situations look like. This protects both parties.
  • ✗
    Watch for the "vendor" frame. If a prospect says "you are my vendor," that's an Explorer disqualifier. Your program is mentor-mentee, not vendor-client. That frame mismatch guarantees conflict.

The 4 Questions That Predict Everything

Based on cross-tier analysis, these four questions — asked naturally during discovery — will predict tier placement with high accuracy.

Question 1
"What have you already tried to solve this problem?"
Architect: Lists specific actions, results, and learnings. Explorer: "I've been exploring" or tells war stories about past wins.
Question 2
"If this works, what does your business look like in 12 months?"
Architect: Specific numbers, team size, lifestyle vision. Explorer: Vague ("making more money") or narrow ("just need one thing").
Question 3
"What's held you back from solving this on your own?"
Architect: "I need the right systems and people around me." Explorer: "The market changed" or "I just need the right tactic."
Question 4
"How would you describe your role in this community if you joined?"
Architect: "I'd share what's working, learn from others, maybe mentor." Explorer: "I'd want to get [specific thing] out of it."
